Q1 — Mandatory
Do you observe a clear compute-optimal trade-off between model size and data size? 
Does the Chinchilla ratio of ~20 tokens per parameter hold at this scale?

A clear compute-optimal trade-off is visible, but the Chinchilla ratio of ~20x does not hold at this scale.
All models perform best at 100% of the training data, which is the expected result given that the dataset acts as a hard ceiling. 
The observed tokens-per-parameter ratios are far below 20x: XS achieves 8.42x, S only 1.21x, M 0.09x, and L a mere 0.04x. 
The primary reason is dataset size — Tiny Shakespeare contains only ~1 million tokens, whereas the Chinchilla ratio was derived from experiments using hundreds of billions of tokens. 
Larger models such as M (10.7M params) and L (25.3M params) would require approximately 214M and 506M tokens respectively for compute-optimal training, which this dataset cannot provide. 
The optimal strategy at this scale is therefore always to use all available data, regardless of model size.



Q2 — Mandatory
At which model sizes do you see clear overfitting? How does overfitting interact with dataset size?

Severe overfitting is present in M and L across all dataset fractions. 
The M model at 10% data reaches a training loss of 0.048 against a validation loss of 4.690 — a gap of +4.64, indicating near-complete memorisation of the training subset. 
L is even more extreme, with a gap of +5.09 at 10% data. XS and S show moderate, manageable overfitting: the largest gap for XS is +0.33 at 10% data, and for S it is +0.71. 
Dataset size strongly reduces overfitting in all models — increasing from 10% to 100% data cuts the gap for M from +4.64 to +1.43, and for S from +0.71 to +0.15. 
This confirms that overfitting is primarily a consequence of model capacity far exceeding the available training data, and that more data is the most effective remedy at this scale.



Q3 — Mandatory
What are the key limitations of running scaling-law experiments at this scale? Which conclusions would you trust to generalise?

The most critical limitation is dataset size: 
Tiny Shakespeare's ~1 million tokens makes it impossible to train M or L models anywhere near their compute-optimal regime, 
meaning the scaling curves are dominated by overfitting rather than true loss scaling. 
Additionally, character-level tokenisation produces a vocabulary of only 65 symbols compared to ~50,000 BPE tokens in large-scale experiments, 
which changes the nature of the learning problem significantly. 
The 6ND FLOP approximation also becomes less accurate at very small N and D, where constant factors ignored by the formula matter more. 
Despite these limitations, two conclusions appear trustworthy enough to generalise: 
(1) validation loss consistently decreases as dataset size increases, which is a robust finding across all scales; 
and (2) smaller models generalise better under data-constrained regimes, 
which directly mirrors the Chinchilla insight even if the exact optimal ratio differs.
